# Lead Releases in Vermont: Summary of TRI Data, 1987--2024

**Data source:** EPA Toxics Release Inventory (TRI)
**Scope:** All facilities in Vermont reporting lead or lead compound releases, 1987--2024 (429 facility-year records)

---

## Key Findings

- **36 facilities** have reported lead or lead compound releases to the TRI over the 38-year period. 13 facilities reported in 2024; 21 have gone inactive.

- **Releases are highly concentrated geographically.** Chittenden County accounts for 72% of cumulative releases; Rutland County accounts for 23%. Together, just two counties contain over 95% of all reported lead releases in the state.

- **Two facilities dominate.** The Ethan Allen Firing Range (Jericho) and GlobalFoundries (Essex Junction) together account for 69% of all cumulative releases statewide. The firing range alone has released over 208,000 pounds of lead since it began TRI reporting in 2007.

- **Releases have declined over time** but remain substantial. The annual average fell from roughly 17,300 lbs/year in the 1990s to 12,200 lbs/year in the 2020s. The peak year was 1999 (49,100 lbs). In 2024, facilities reported 13,400 lbs total.

- **Industry mix spans 14 sectors**, including electrical equipment, transportation equipment, computers/electronics, chemicals, and the military (firing ranges). The two largest emitters are a military installation and a semiconductor fabrication plant.

## Limitations

TRI data measure **reported releases**, not environmental contamination or human exposure. Several important caveats apply. First, TRI only covers facilities above reporting thresholds (generally 10+ employees and specific chemical usage levels), so smaller sources of lead contamination---including aging residential paint, drinking water infrastructure, and small industrial operations---are invisible in these data. Second, "total releases" aggregate air emissions, water discharges, and land disposal, which have very different exposure pathways and health implications. A pound of lead released into a river is not equivalent to a pound deposited in a contained landfill. Third, TRI is self-reported by facilities and subject to measurement and reporting inconsistencies over time. Changes in reporting requirements (e.g., the addition of lead compounds to reporting categories in later years) can create apparent trends that reflect regulatory changes rather than real emission changes. Finally, the geographic coordinates identify *facility locations*, not where contamination actually reaches people---groundwater plumes, air dispersion, and downstream water contamination can affect communities far from the point of release.

## Next Steps

Several lines of investigation would complement this TRI analysis. Overlaying facility locations with demographic data (Census tract income, race/ethnicity, age distributions) would allow a preliminary environmental justice screening---are lead-releasing facilities disproportionately located near disadvantaged communities? Linking TRI release data to EPA Superfund or brownfield site designations could identify facilities where releases have led to documented contamination requiring cleanup. For the Ethan Allen Firing Range specifically, soil and groundwater sampling data from the Department of Defense would provide a much clearer picture of actual contamination than release totals alone. Finally, comparing Vermont's TRI lead release trends with neighboring states (NH, NY, MA) would contextualize whether Vermont's trajectory is typical or unusual for the region.
